Video Editing Training Program

Role: Instructional Designer & Facilitator

Tools: Camtasia Zoom Instructional Design Facilitation

Project Overview

I designed and facilitated a hands-on training session for a team of Business Learning Specialists focused on building foundational video editing skills using the application Camtasia. The session emphasized practical application, essential functionality, and confidence-building for teammates who created internal training content.

Rather than attempting to cover the full breadth of Camtasia's capabilities, the training prioritized core editing workflows that directly supported the team's instructional design needs.

[Session] Camtasia Video Editing Training — Initialized
[Format] Guided, follow-along hands-on workshop
[Audience] Business Learning Specialists (mixed experience levels)
[Goal] Build a shared baseline of video editing capability

Instructional Design Strategy

1. Design for Application, Not Just Awareness

The session was structured as a guided, follow-along experience, with learners actively working in Camtasia throughout. Pre-work reduced friction, and each concept included an immediate opportunity to apply concepts — learners practiced in real time alongside demonstrations.

Rationale: Skill-based tool training is most effective when learners perform tasks during instruction rather than observe passively.

2. Chunking Content into Real-World Workflows

Content was sequenced into progressive, task-based chunks aligned to how video editing actually occurs: workspace orientation → object creation and manipulation → text, markers, and annotations → audio and video editing → timeline management → exporting and publishing.

Rationale: Chunking reduces cognitive load and helps learners integrate individual features into a coherent workflow.

3. Essentials First, Advanced Concepts Later

The training emphasized high-impact, frequently used features — splitting, moving, resizing, cropping, audio cleanup, voiceover basics, timeline navigation, and exporting best practices. Advanced features such as animations, effects, and SCORM output were positioned as optional extensions.

Rationale: Focusing on essentials enables immediate application while preventing learners being overwhelmed.

4. Scaffolded Exploration and Extension

The session included structured opportunities for exploration: "if time allows" segments introduced higher-level features, intermediate learners could explore enhancements independently, and advanced capabilities were framed as next steps for continued learning.

Rationale: This approach supports varied experience levels while maintaining a clear baseline of required skills.

5. Real-World Editing Scenarios

Instruction was grounded in authentic use cases: editing Zoom and SME recordings, removing filler words from audio, splitting and reordering content, syncing audio and visuals, and obscuring sensitive information using blur, crop, zoom, and overlays.

Rationale: Aligning training to real deliverables increases relevance and transfer to job performance.

6. Intentional Environment Setup

Because video editing is highly visual, the session incorporated environment design guidance: dual-monitor recommendations, dedicated screens for Camtasia and Zoom, and clear pre-session setup expectations to reduce technical friction on day one.

Rationale: Reducing technical friction allows learners to focus on skill development rather than navigation.

7. Reinforcement of Productive Editing Behaviors

The training emphasized habits that build efficiency and confidence: saving early and often, using undo/redo frequently, zooming the timeline to manage complexity, and using hover states for quick context.

Rationale: These behaviors reduce frustration and help learners work more independently over time.

Challenges & Solutions

The Hurdle: Mixed experience levels within a single session — from complete beginners to moderately experienced users.

The Fix: I structured the session with layered entry points, sequencing content from foundational concepts while building in extension opportunities to keep intermediate users engaged without leaving beginners behind.

The Hurdle: In pre-assessment surveys, learners had expressed anxiety about using technology in general and editing video in particular; this complexity threatened to impact engagement and confidence.

The Fix: I incorporated differentiated pacing and psychological safety — emphasizing low-risk actions like undo/redo and frequent saves, and providing targeted support to beginners during foundational tasks.

The Hurdle: Balancing differentiated pacing in real time as learners progressed at varying speeds.

The Fix: I facilitated the session as a fluid, responsive experience rather than a rigid progression — using built-in pauses, checkpoints, and independent exploration windows to meet learners where they were.

The Hurdle: The team's work centers on updating system-focused training content, not producing stylized videos — advanced features risked being irrelevant noise.

The Fix: I intentionally prioritized functional editing skills tied directly to core deliverables: editing existing recordings, ensuring clarity and accuracy, and minimizing time spent on advanced visual effects.

Outcomes & Skills

  • Needs Analysis: Identified team-specific use cases (Zoom/SME recordings, sensitive content) to scope training appropriately.
  • Instructional Design: Applied chunking, scaffolding, and application-first principles to structure a cohesive learning experience.
  • Facilitation: Delivered a live, hands-on session with real-time responsiveness to mixed experience levels and learner anxiety.
  • Differentiation: Designed layered content to ensure accessibility for beginners while providing depth for intermediate learners.
  • Tool Expertise: Leveraged deep Camtasia knowledge to connect features directly to practical editing workflows.

Final Result: By the end of the session, learners could navigate the Camtasia interface with confidence, perform essential video and audio editing tasks, structure content on a timeline, and export usable training deliverables — establishing a shared baseline of video editing capability across the team. By the end of the session, each team member was tasked creating a two minute proof-of-concept that they could use as a model for future projects.

Outcome: Several participants reported publishing their first video(s) within days of completing the training. Before the training, only a handful of team members could produce/edit a video on relatively short notice. After the session, the team's dependency on others to produce video content had been significantly reduced.
